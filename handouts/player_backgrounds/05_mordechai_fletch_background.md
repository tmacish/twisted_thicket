# Mordechai Fletch
### Human Magic-User — Your Background

---

You have published two monographs. The first: *On the Theory of Consciousness Transfer and Its Known Failure Modes*. The second: *Fragmented Presence: What Partial Transfers Leave Behind*. You are working on a third. Your area of expertise is the wizardly ambition of copying a living mind into a new vessel — the theory, the mathematics, the history of attempts, and, most importantly, the ways it goes catastrophically wrong.

You know the theory is sound in principle. You know it is catastrophically dangerous in practice. You have spent twelve years studying the failure modes precisely so that you understand what not to do, and because the failure modes are, frankly, more interesting than the successes.

When reports of a substance that compels carving a specific wizard's initial reached the Critwall scholarly community, you were one of three people in the room who understood immediately what they were hearing. You were the only one who got up and started packing.

Someone is doing badly what you have spent twelve years studying how to do properly. This offends you professionally.

---

### What You Believe You Will Find

The substance — called *white plume dust* in less careful conversation — is not an alchemical drug. You are almost certain of this without having seen it yet. The compulsion it produces is too specific, too architecturally precise, to be an accident of chemistry. A substance that drives people to carve one specific letter while muttering what bystanders describe as a name is carrying directed information. Someone's intention has been encoded into a physical medium.

The most likely mechanism: a failed or partial consciousness transfer, where the target vessel was not a mind or a body but a replicable substance. The traces of the original consciousness survive in the dust, degraded and incoherent, but persistent enough to express a compulsion.

If you are right, whoever made this was attempting something extraordinary. The fact that it is being distributed through criminal networks in a forest on the Shield Lands frontier suggests the attempt did not go as intended.

---

### The Coach Contract

You needed to get to Ringstown. The fastest route was the main road from Critwall. A gnome named **Blink Tinderflip** was hiring riders for an escort job going the right direction. The pay was acceptable, the timing was right, and you had no particular interest in the cargo — sealed crates, someone's sensitive shipment, not your concern.

---

### What Happened on the Road

The creature that came out of the Thicket was bioluminescent. Cold white light, not heat-based. You noted this because bioluminescence in a land predator of that size is either a natural adaptation you have never encountered, or an effect of something external applied to the animal. Given where you are going and what you believe you will find, you consider the second explanation more likely.

You survived. You are now at the Ringstown gate. You are already composing the opening section of the third monograph.

---

### What You Know About the Region

**Keraptis.** The name is relevant. He was a wizard of significant power, active perhaps a century and a half ago, associated with a mountain north of the Shield Lands. He is believed dead, but the scholarly literature on his work is thin and, in your view, credulous — most accounts focus on the legend rather than the methodology.

The methodology is what interests you. If the consciousness-transfer theory is correct, Keraptis may have attempted what you have been studying. The *K* that dust victims carve could be the residual signature of a partial transfer, an identity fragment that survived in the medium. Or it could be something else. You intend to find out.

**White Plume Mountain** is north. It smokes. You have not been near it. You have begun to think you may need to go.

---

### Who You Are

You narrate what you are observing while you observe it. People find this useful approximately 60% of the time and annoying the other 40%. You consider this an acceptable ratio.

You are professionally, specifically concerned about anyone who uses the dust. You know exactly what it is doing to their mind — or at least, you have a working hypothesis — and the hypothesis is not reassuring. You will explain this in more detail than anyone has asked for.

You will not use the dust yourself. Not under any circumstances. Not even to confirm your hypothesis. You know precisely what the hypothesis predicts will happen to the mind under prolonged exposure, and the answer is not something you are willing to experience in order to be thorough.
