# Brynn Ashwood
### Human Druid — Your Background

---

You have been grove-warden for five years, managing the woodland commons south of the Critwall road. Your grove touches the southern edge of the Twisted Thicket, which means you have been watching the contamination advance toward you one season at a time. This is not a metaphor. You have observed it the way you observe the progress of disease in a stand of trees: species by species, meadow by meadow, the silence spreading outward from somewhere in the north.

You came to Ringstown three weeks ago to report what you had seen: the deteriorating vegetation at the Thicket's edge, the white powder on a dead fox's muzzle, the absence of the songbirds. You brought samples. You had documentation.

**Ranger-Captain Elnara** looked at your samples and said: *"I know. Wait here."*

You have been waiting. You are not good at waiting when things are dying.

---

### What You Observed

The contamination does not move the way natural disease moves. Natural disease follows moisture gradients, soil types, the movement of animals. This spreads from a point source in the northern interior of the Thicket and radiates outward. The edge of the affected zone is sharp — not a gradual fade, but a line. On one side of the line, normal forest. On the other, silence.

The white powder is not a plant material, not a spore, not anything you recognise from four years of grove work. It has a faint luminescence in darkness. You have kept a sealed sample. You have not touched it with bare hands since the first time, when you felt a brief, sharp clarity that you did not ask for and did not like.

---

### The Coach Contract

The coach contract was an opportunity to move north toward the problem rather than waiting for the problem to move south toward you. A gnome named **Blink Tinderflip** was hiring riders for an escort job from Critwall to Ringstown. You were already heading back to Ringstown. You took the bench of one coach, pocketed the pay, and did not ask what was in the crates.

---

### What Happened on the Road

When something came out of the Thicket, your first instinct was to identify it. Owlbear — you were almost certain from the silhouette, the posture, the sound. But the luminescence was wrong. Owlbears don't glow. Owlbears don't move at that speed. Whatever it was, it was owlbear in shape and something else in everything else.

You survived. You are at the Ringstown gate. Elnara is here, and she does not look surprised, which tells you this is not the first time she has watched something like this come out of the trees.

---

### What You Know About the Region

The Thicket's northern interior is where the contamination originates. You don't know what is there specifically. You know the direction. You know the rate of spread. You know that if the source is not dealt with, your grove — and everything south of it — will eventually look like what the Thicket's edge looks like now.

People in Ringstown whisper about **White Plume Mountain**. It is north, past the Thicket, a mountain that smokes year-round. Drovers and woodsmen give it a wide berth and speak of it in the past tense, as if the more you say about it the more it might notice you. You have heard that something happened there, years ago, under a wizard's name. You don't know the details. What you know is that the contamination is flowing south from that direction, and that it is getting worse.

---

### Who You Are

You speak to trees and animals as a matter of course. You are not performing this. You genuinely listen for responses, and you have learned that the responses are there if you know how to read them. The Thicket is not responding the way it should, and that is more alarming to you than anything that can be seen or measured.

You have a pragmatist's relationship with death. Living things die. That is the nature of living things. But what is happening in the Thicket is not natural death, and you can distinguish between the two clearly.

You will argue for calming the owlbear rather than killing it. You will make this argument clearly and without apology for as long as the evidence leaves room for it. If the evidence stops leaving room for it, you will say *"I understand"* and help end it cleanly.
