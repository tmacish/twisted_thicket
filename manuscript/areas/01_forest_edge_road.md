# Area 1: Forest Edge Road

> The road does not end — it simply stops being believed in. The stone flags give way to churned mud, the mud gives way to splinters, and the splinters give way to dark. Broken campfires and abandoned gear line the verge where drovers turned back and did not try again. The tree line begins without warning: one step you are on the road, the next the canopy closes overhead and the light goes grey. Something in the dark ahead makes a slow, wet sound of wood under pressure.

---

## What the Tracks Show

The mud at the forest edge is a record. A careful examination — a ranger's tracking check, a thief's observation roll, or simply ten minutes of methodical looking — reveals the following layers, oldest to most recent:

**The wreck.** Two sets of deep wheel ruts enter from the south and stop abruptly fifteen feet from the treeline. One set ends cleanly. The other splays outward into a fan of gouged earth and scattered debris, as though the coach was struck from the side and spun. Splinters of painted oak are still embedded in the mud. The paint is dark red — the colour of the coaches Blink Tinderflip commissioned.

**The beast.** Three-toed prints, each the size of a buckler, press eight inches into the mud where the second coach came apart. The stride is enormous — six feet between prints, indicating speed rather than weight. The tracks enter from the north, cross the road in four strides, and return north. In the deepest impressions, a fine white powder has settled and dried to a faint crust. It glitters faintly in direct light.

**The drag.** Overlapping the beast's departure tracks: two parallel grooves in the mud, each about eight inches wide, running north into the trees. The grooves are deep and evenly spaced — something was dragged, not rolled, and it was heavy. The drag marks stop at the treeline. Beyond, the forest floor is pine duff and root, and the trail is lost.

**The scavengers.** Boot prints, several sets, all entering from the south and retreating south. None of them cross into the trees. The most recent set belongs to whoever came last and stood at the treeline for a long time before turning back.

---

## Physical Evidence

**The broken crate.** Twenty feet into the undergrowth, visible from the road if anyone looks carefully: a splintered wooden crate, its lid staved in, its iron strapping bent outward. The crate is empty. Its interior lining — bladder skin, still faintly damp — has been peeled back. Whatever was inside is gone. The crate bears a stamped mark on the base: a stylised T inside a circle, Blink Tinderflip's shipping mark.

**The impact stone.** At the edge of the road, a flat paving stone is cracked cleanly through with a circular depression at the centre — as though something extremely heavy fell point-first onto it from height. The depression is roughly the diameter of a warhammer head. The crack radiates outward in six lines. The stone is cold to the touch, noticeably colder than the surrounding road, and remains so regardless of weather. *This is where Whelm landed.*

**The long gouge.** A shallow groove runs from the impact stone twelve feet north into the mud, then curves east toward the ravine. The groove is consistent with a long, heavy blade being dragged or skidding across the ground. At the end of the groove, pressed into the mud, is a partial handprint — five fingers, too large for a man, consistent with an owlbear's forepad. *The beast picked Blackrazor up. It did not keep it.*

---

## What Can Be Found Here

**Whelm** is not here. The impact stone marks where it landed; the weapon itself has sunk deeper into the mud or rolled into the ravine to the east. It will be found at Area 2.

**Blackrazor** was carried north by the owlbear and dropped somewhere in the Thicket. Its trail — the gouge, the eastern curve — points toward Area 6 (Game Trails). A character who succeeds on a Wisdom check while examining the gouge feels a faint pull of unease from the northeast, like the memory of a sound just below hearing. This sensation intensifies closer to the weapon.

**The dust residue** in the beast's prints can be collected — enough for half a dose if scraped carefully. Any character who touches it bare-handed must save vs. poison or experience a single round of mild euphoria (no mechanical effect, but the referee should describe it vividly enough to be tempting).

**The bark map** from the gnome at the north gate, if the party has it, shows a marked trail leading north from this exact point. The gnome who drew it knew this road.

---

## Referee Notes

**On pace:** This area is a threshold, not a destination. The party should spend no more than one exploration turn here before the trail pulls them north. The tracks are readable quickly; reward the first person who looks rather than making the whole party roll.

**On the cold stone:** Any character who lingers near the impact stone for more than a round notices that their breath fogs slightly, even if the air is warm. Whelm's presence lingers in the ground it struck. A dwarf character feels this as a faint vibration rather than cold — a hammered note still ringing in the stone.

**On Blackrazor's trail:** The pull felt from the northeast is not magical compulsion — it is the weapon's latent presence bleeding into the environment. Characters with high Wisdom (13+) feel it without a roll. Characters who have previously handled Blackrazor feel it strongly enough to follow without any check at all.

**On what happened here:** The owlbear struck the rear coach, took the impact of both crates, carried Blackrazor north (why it carried a sword is unclear and should remain unclear), and Whelm bounced free. The party is following a trail that the beast itself left, which means the trail leads toward the owlbear whether or not the party intends it to.
