# Area 4: Dust Pool Glade

> The forest opens into a clearing that should be beautiful and is not. The glade is the right size and shape for beauty, a gap in the canopy, afternoon light coming through in columns, a shallow basin at the centre ringed with smooth stones, but everything aimed at the basin is wrong. The flowers lean toward it on stems bent at angles that have nothing to do with the light. The bark on the surrounding trees has split open along lines that, at a certain distance, almost resolve into letters. The pool itself glows white-blue even in direct sunlight, and the smell rising from it is cold, bitter, and faintly sweet in a way that makes the back of the throat tighten. Nothing is making noise. No insects. No wind. No birds. The silence is absolute and has been for some time.

---

## The Glade

The clearing is roughly forty feet across, almost circular, as though something burned or cleared it long ago and the forest never fully reclaimed it. The treeline is tight on all sides, and the ground between the treeline and the pool basin, perhaps twenty feet in any direction, is wrong in ways the party can identify if they look:

**The vegetation.** Every plant in the glade is oriented toward the pool. Not leaning, *oriented*, the way a compass points north, with the same indifference to what should be influencing it. Flowers have opened on the wrong faces of their stems. Two saplings near the east edge have grown in arcs, their trunks curved, their tops pointing at the basin. A patch of moss on the north stones has died in a rough circle, the dead fringe still reaching inward. A character who examines the bark of the surrounding trees closely finds partial letters, not carved, grown into the bark during the tree's own expansion. They are not consistent across trees. They are not quite readable. They have a K in them, always.

**The silence.** There are no insects, no birds, no small-creature rustling. A Ranger or Druid who stands at the treeline for one round understands instinctively that this silence is not fear, animals are not hiding here, they have not been here in some time. Something about the pool's chemistry has altered the environment at a fundamental level. The party's animals, if any, will refuse to enter the clearing. They cannot be forced; they will injure themselves rather than cross the treeline.

**The cold.** The glade is three to five degrees colder than the surrounding forest. There is no meteorological explanation. The cold intensifies near the pool, within five feet of the basin, breath fogs. The stones around the basin rim have frost on them, despite the season.

---

## The Pool

The basin is twelve feet across, the water perhaps eighteen inches at its deepest. The glow is consistent, white-blue, faint but unmistakeable in shadow, and still visible in direct daylight as a shimmer at odds with normal water's behaviour. The water itself is clear, and through it the stone basin floor is visible. The floor of the basin is coated in a thin white deposit, crystallised dust that has settled and hardened into a layer perhaps half an inch thick. This is not algae or mineral deposit. A Magic-User who examines it (without touching) recognises it as residue from a prolonged magical contamination, the source running continuously into this water for long enough that the accumulation is structural.

**The contamination's source.** The pool is fed by a spring from below. The spring is natural; the contamination is not. Old Brindle (if present) can explain, and a Ranger or Druid who examines the basin and succeeds on a Wisdom check understands: the dust is entering through the groundwater from the north, seeping through stone from something underground. It is not being produced here. The pool is an endpoint, not an origin. Destroying the pool cleans this location; it does not stop the source.

**What the pool does to people who touch it.** A character who touches the water bare-handed must save versus spells or feel an immediate, pleasant warmth in the hand spreading up the arm, the dust's euphoric effect, delivered transdermally at low dose. On a failed save, the character gains a mild compulsion to drink from the pool (save versus poison to resist; refusing the compulsion is possible and costs no mechanical resource but should be described). A character who drinks gains the dust's combat benefits for 5 rounds (half duration, half dose) and must roll on the backlash table at the end, with a +2 bonus to the roll (making the severe results less likely but not impossible). Touching the water while wearing gloves: no effect. Wading into it barefoot: treat as two simultaneous bare-hand contacts.

**What the pool shows.** The water's surface does not reflect the sky correctly. A character who looks closely into the pool and succeeds on a Wisdom check (or simply stares for more than one round) notices the reflection showing something other than the canopy overhead, something darker, an impression of depth that has no source in the physical basin. If the false Keraptis is actively watching through the pool (referee's discretion; he watches during the cleansing rite automatically), the reflection briefly resolves into a face: narrow, patient, with the eyes of someone who has been waiting a very long time and is accustomed to it. The face does not react to being seen. It simply observes. Old Brindle will break contact immediately if he sees a character staring into the pool too long.

---

## Old Brindle

He is here. He has been here, without sleep, for over two days, driven back three nights ago when the owlbear came to drink and returned the following morning. He has not left the glade since.

Old Brindle is seated at the northwest edge of the basin on a flat stone he dragged in from the treeline. He has a druidic focus, a fork of ash wood, wrapped in cord, in his hands, and he is muttering. He looks up when the party enters the glade with the unhurried calm of a man who has been expecting them, which is approximately true: the forest does not speak to druids in words, but it indicates, and the indication has been toward the south for the past hour.

He is not well. Three nights ago the owlbear came to drink and its arrival disrupted the partial ward he had placed; the backlash cost him. He has 6 hit points remaining. He has used all his prepared spells except *cure light wounds* (1), which he is saving for an emergency. He is maintaining the partial ward on willpower alone, and willpower, as he will tell the party if asked, is not a renewable resource.

> *"I know what you want. You want me to tell you where the beast is. You want a map of the situation, a set of clear steps. So do I."*
>
> *"The pool is not the source. It is a mouth. Whatever is upstream, north, underground, has been feeding this water for months, possibly longer. I can close the mouth. I have been trying to close the mouth. What I cannot do alone is hold it open long enough to finish the rite. The pool pushes back."*

**What Old Brindle knows:**
- The pool is a scrying conduit. Something intelligent is watching through it. He calls it *the voice in the water* and will not use the name Keraptis without visible reluctance; if the party uses it, he says: *"That name has weight. Don't use it carelessly near the pool."*
- The owlbear comes to this pool at night to drink. This is the cycle: the pool contaminates the creature, the creature's own dust-saturated body seeps into the water when it drinks, which intensifies the pool, which intensifies the creature's contamination. Three nights ago the owlbear was more contaminated than Old Brindle had ever seen it, and its visit fed the pool enough to break his partial ward.
- The cleansing rite will close the pool, kill the glow, neutralise the crystallised deposit, break the scrying connection. It will not stop the source. It will stop the owlbear's reinforcement cycle, which means the creature's dust level will slowly decrease rather than continuously increase. This gives the party a better chance at Area 5.
- The K-marks in the trees around the glade were not here when Old Brindle arrived. They appeared the morning after the owlbear's most recent visit. He does not know who made them. He has the same interpretation as the party should at this point: someone is directing the owlbear's movements, or at least marking them.
- The rumour that he can call the owlbear off is false. *"I can speak to animals. The owlbear is no longer an animal in the way the word means. Something is riding in its head that is not animal and not natural. I spoke to it once. What answered me was not the beast."*

**What Old Brindle will not say** unless he trusts the party: he attempted to make contact with the voice in the water once, deliberately, to understand what he was dealing with. It answered. It knew his name. It knew his niece's name. (He has no niece, Yenna Brindle is his niece only in the same way the town calls her that, by adoption of neighbourhood, but the voice knew the name, which means it has been watching Ringstown through the pool for some time.) He has not told Yenna any of this. He does not intend to.

---

## The Cleansing Rite

Old Brindle can perform a druidic cleansing of the pool. He needs two things he cannot provide alone:

**First: an anchor.** Something brought from outside the Thicket's contaminated zone, untouched by dust, naturally pure. The bark map from the north gate gnome qualifies (the gnome marked it before the contamination intensified; it carries her uncorrupted intention). A flask of water from the Ringstown well qualifies. Holy water from Brother Aldric at the shrine qualifies and is the most powerful option. A handful of earth from the road south of the treeline, taken before entering the Thicket, qualifies. If the party has none of these, Old Brindle has one: a sprig of heather from outside the Thicket, gathered at the forest edge before he entered two days ago. He brought it deliberately, knowing the rite would eventually need an anchor. It is slightly wilted but still intact and still qualifies. He offers it without hesitation the moment the party asks. A referee who worries this is too easy should consider that Old Brindle has been here for two days with 6 hit points specifically so that this rite can happen; the anchor was never the obstacle. The anchor is not destroyed by the rite; it is returned to its holder afterward, unchanged.

**Second: someone to hold the connection open.** Old Brindle must complete the rite in one uninterrupted turn (ten rounds). During that turn, the pool will resist, it pushes back, attempting to break the druid's concentration. Old Brindle can hold concentration against ordinary distraction, but the pool's resistance manifests as a physical pressure: anyone within ten feet of the basin during the rite must save versus spells each round or feel a strong compulsion to break Old Brindle's focus (shout, grab his arm, flee the clearing). This is not a charm, it is environmental psychic pressure, and it affects the party regardless of their intentions. A character who fails their save must make a Wisdom check to override the compulsion; success is possible but costs an action. A character who chooses to *hold the pool's attention*, actively engaging with the water, staring into it, speaking to whatever is watching, can absorb the pressure on behalf of the group, but they will make eye contact with the voice in the water.

**The rite's completion.** If Old Brindle finishes the rite uninterrupted:
- The pool's glow extinguishes. The white-blue light drains in a spiral over one round and is gone.
- The crystallised deposit on the basin floor goes grey and begins to dissolve.
- The cold disperses over one turn. The insects return within the hour.
- The owlbear's *K-Whispers* ability is suppressed for the remainder of the session, the pool was amplifying it, and without the pool the aura loses its range.
- Old Brindle collapses. He is not dead, but he is unconscious and will remain so for 1d4 hours. He cannot be roused early. He should be left somewhere safe.

**If the rite is interrupted.** The pool releases the held pressure in a burst. Everyone within thirty feet takes 2d6 cold damage (save versus breath for half). Old Brindle is thrown back and is at 0 hit points (dying; he can be stabilised). The pool's glow intensifies for 1d6 hours before returning to normal. The false Keraptis has seen the party clearly.

## What's Here

**The basin stones.** The smooth stones ringing the pool were placed deliberately, not deposited by water action. They are river stones from a source some distance north, a druid or ranger can identify them as mountain granite, not the local limestone. They are spaced at intervals consistent with a ritual circle predating the contamination by decades. Someone made this pool a sacred site before it was a scrying pool. The rite Old Brindle performs is closing something that was opened by someone else, a long time ago.

**The bone pile.** At the east edge of the glade, half-hidden by the leaning vegetation: a low mound of bones. Mixed species, deer, fox, boar, and several that are identifiable as gnoll. All clean, well-picked, arranged rather than scattered. Animals come to the pool and do not always leave. The most recent bones (a deer) are perhaps a week old. The oldest (the gnoll) are years old and slightly bleached. The gnoll bones predate the owlbear's corruption and suggest the pool has had some draw since long before the dust.

**Old Brindle's camp.** A rolled blanket against the northwest treeline, a clay pot with cold herb tea, a drawstring pouch with three berries (plumeberries, naturally grown, non-contaminated, he gathered them on his way in before the contamination had reached the southern tree line). The plumeberries are not medicinal; they are Old Brindle's food. A character who eats them without asking should have this noted but not punished.

**The view north at dusk.** From the centre of the glade, looking north through the canopy gap, on any clear evening: a plume of pale smoke is visible above the tree line, far north. At dusk specifically, when the light is certain angles, the smoke briefly takes the shape of a face, not rendered carefully, not detailed, but unmistakeable as a face, turned south, watching. It does not linger. Old Brindle has seen it every evening he has been here. He does not comment on it unless asked. *"It has always been there. We just did not always look north at dusk."*

## Referee Notes

**On the pool as a pacing tool.** This is the adventure's breathing room, a place of atmosphere and revelation rather than combat. After the violence of Area 2 and the social pressure of Area 3, the party should feel the weight of what they have walked into. Old Brindle is useful precisely because he is exhausted: he knows things and is too tired to be coy about them. Let him talk. Reward the party for listening.

**On the voice in the water.** If a character chooses to hold the pool's attention during the rite and makes eye contact with the false Keraptis, describe what they see briefly and without explanation: a face, a sense of vast underground space, the impression of being studied the way a naturalist studies a specimen. The false Keraptis does not speak during this contact, he is observing, not recruiting. He now knows this character's face. This has no mechanical effect in this adventure, but a referee running into White Plume Mountain should note which character it was.

**On Old Brindle's recovery.** After the rite, he sleeps in the glade for 1d4 hours. When he wakes, he will tell the party where the owlbear's hollow is (Area 5, north through the timber yard; he scouted it before beginning the pool vigil and knows what is inside). He will also say: *"The beast came here to drink because it could not help itself. Whatever is in it is not its fault. I could not save it. Perhaps you can."* He does not mean kill it, necessarily. He does not know if there is another option. He suspects there is not, and he is sad about it. From the glade, the path east returns to the main north-south trail; from there, the timber yard and Area 5 are directly north.

**On the cycle broken.** If the party destroys or cleans the pool and then faces the owlbear at Area 5, the *K-Whispers* suppression is a meaningful advantage, it removes the area-effect save penalty that makes the owlbear's most dangerous ability chain. A party that skips this area and goes straight to Area 5 will face a harder fight. This is the reward for following the threads rather than going directly north.

**On rumour #92** (*"If you destroy the glowing pool in the Thicket, the owlbear dies. The pool is its heart."*) This is false, and Old Brindle knows it. If the party tests this theory after the rite, Old Brindle says, without inflection: *"The pool was feeding it. Your heart is not your food."*

**If Old Brindle dies.** Old Brindle is the adventure's primary source of context on the pool, the owlbear cycle, and the voice in the water. If he dies before sharing this information, the party is not blocked — they can discover the pool's purpose through examination, the K-marks in the glade, and the evidence in Area 5. What they lose is Old Brindle's emotional framing: the note that the owlbear was not at fault, that the pool's history predates the current corruption, and the offer of hope for the beast. The rite itself cannot be completed without him unless the party includes a druid of sufficient level (Druid 5 or higher) who can improvise the cleansing with a successful Wisdom check.
